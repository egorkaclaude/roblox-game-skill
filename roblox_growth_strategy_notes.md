# Roblox Game Growth Strategy — Condensed Notes

## How the Roblox Algorithm Actually Works

Roblox doesn't recommend games based on how "fun" they are in the abstract. It recommends games based on three measurable numbers:

1. **Engagement** — how long people stay per session. Aim for 10+ minute sessions as a starting benchmark.
2. **Retention** — do players come back? Day-one retention is the critical early signal; most struggling games are weak here.
3. **Monetization** — what percentage of players spend, and how much. This is what Roblox itself profits from, so it's weighted heavily.

Roblox runs on a **rolling seven-day data window**. Changes don't show results overnight — you need a full week (sometimes two) of new data before the algorithm re-evaluates your game and before you can judge whether a change actually worked. Don't make snap judgments after a day.

**The core strategic loop:**
- Check your analytics.
- Identify your single *worst* metric (engagement, retention, or monetization).
- Fix only that one thing.
- Wait a full week.
- Re-check and repeat.

Don't try to fix everything at once — fix the weakest link, since that's what's holding the game back from the next tier of visibility.

## Building the Game Itself

**1. Build on proven concepts, not personal inspiration.**
Look at what's currently trending/working in a genre and build your own twist on it, rather than building something original "you saw in a dream." Proven concept + your own twist beats a novel idea with no market validation.

**2. The icon is the first impression — and it matters enormously.**
Nobody plays a game they don't click. The icon needs to be clear, readable at small size, and stand out visually against competitors. A unique/curious icon often outperforms a flashy generic one — curiosity drives clicks (this is measured as "playthrough rate" — the % of people who click your game after seeing it).

**3. Win the first 30 seconds.**
No long intros, no long tutorials. Players judge almost instantly and will leave if bored. Drop them into the actual fun part of the game immediately — front-load the most satisfying/engaging content.

**4. Build a strong core loop.**
The core loop = the repeatable action-reward cycle a player does over and over (action → reward → upgrade/progress → repeat, possibly including "rebirth"/prestige systems). This loop is what makes a game feel addictive and gives players a reason to return. This is the single biggest lever for retention.

**5. Ship an MVP, not a "finished" game.**
Release the smallest playable version with the core loop working, rather than waiting until every planned feature is built. This lets you find out fast whether the concept works at all before investing further. If it's not working, that's a signal to iterate or pivot — not to keep polishing indefinitely.

## What NOT to Do

- **Don't over-invest in graphics for retention.** Visual polish doesn't reliably drive retention — many top "brain rot"-style games use simple, low-effort art styles and still perform extremely well. Graphics are not the priority lever.
- **Don't throw ad spend at a retention/engagement problem.** Ads bring cheap clicks, not necessarily players who stay or spend — running ads on an unoptimized game just feeds the algorithm bad data (people arriving and immediately leaving). Ads are useful *only* for seeding initial data/players so Roblox has something to evaluate — not as a fix for a weak game.
- **Don't obsess over data day-to-day.** Because of the 7-day rolling window, judging a change after 1 day is meaningless. Be patient and wait for a full data cycle.
- **Don't assume a flopped game means a bad idea.** It's often the thumbnail, the core loop, or a specific mechanic — not the concept itself. Diagnose before abandoning.
- **Don't build in a vacuum.** Validate against what's already proven to work in the genre rather than working purely from personal taste.
